Engineering and Science Programs

CTAE/ Career pathway: Engineering and Technology

The engineering pathway gives students the basic skills and concepts needed in all fields of engineering. Students learn to work with various resources, technology, and peers to enhance creativity, the application of science and math skills, and become more proficient in logical problem solving. Teachers: Mr. Tom Wickson and Ms. Liadn Zhou Courses: Foundations of Engineering Survey Of Engineering Concepts Application of Engineering and Technology (Optional)Engineering Research Design Project Management Video made by teachers for Engineering All students in the Engineering Pathway are given the opportunity to compete in TSA. Also compete in competitive robotics (VEX, ECar, FIRST, BEST)

Science Courses: Physical Science: A general course of chemistry and physics. Include concepts like the conceptualization of the structure of atoms, motion and forces, and the conservation of energy and matter, the action/reaction principle, and wave behavior.

Biology and honors biology: Continuation of life sciences in K-8 and give students the skills to be proficient in biology. It includes concepts like the interdependence of organisms, the relationship of matter, energy, and organization in living systems, the behavior of organisms, and biological evolution.

Chemistry and Honors Chemistry: Continuation of the physical sciences in K-8 and gives students the skills to be proficient in chemistry. It has concepts like the structure of atoms, structure and properties of matter, and the conservation and interaction of energy and matter.

Physics: Continuation of the physical sciences in K-8 and gives students the skills to be proficient in physics. This includes more abstract concepts like the interactions of matter and energy, velocity, acceleration, force, energy, momentum, and charge.

Human Anatomy/physiology: Continuation of high school biology and investigations started in K-8. Areas of study include organization of the body; protection, support and movement; providing internal coordination and regulation; processing and transporting; and reproduction, growth and development. The course integrates careers related to medicine, research, health-care and modern medical technology and utilizes case studies concerning diseases, disorders and ailments.

Epidemiology: Extends student investigations that begin in Biology. It integrates scientific investigations using real world situations to find patterns and determine causation of pathological conditions.

Environmental Science: Continuation of environmental science learnt in K-8. It integrates the study of many components of our environment, including the human impact on our planet.

Earth Systems: Continuation of student investigations that began in K-8 Earth Science and Life Science and investigate the connections among Earth’s systems through Earth history.

Forensic science: Designed to build upon science concepts and to apply science to the investigation of crime scenes.

AP Biology: The major themes of the course include molecules and cells, heredity and evolution, and organisms and populations.

AP Chemistry: The major themes for this course are the structure of matter, the states of matter, reactions, descriptive chemistry, and college level chemistry laboratories.

AP Physics I: The equivalent of a first-semester college course in algebra-based physics. The course covers Newtonian mechanics; work, energy, and power; and mechanical waves and sound. It will also introduce electric circuits.

AP Physics C: A calculus based course that includes kinematics; Newton’s laws of motion; work, energy, and power; systems of particles and linear momentum; circular motion and rotation; and oscillations and gravitation.

AP Environmental Science: The major themes for this course include Earth systems and resources, the living world, populations, land and water use, energy resources and consumption, pollution, and global change.

IB Biology HL - Year one and two: Major topics for the first year of this course include statistical analysis, cells, the chemistry of life, nucleic acids and proteins, cellular respiration, photosynthesis, genetics and biotechnology. Major topics for year two include plant science, ecology, evolution, and human health and physiology.

IB Chemistry SL: Major topics for this course are atomic structure, periodicity, bonding, energetics, kinetics, equilibrium, acids and bases, oxidation and reduction, organic chemistry, measurement techniques, and data processing. Optional topics include modern analytical chemistry, human biochemistry, chemistry in industry and technology, medicines and drugs, environmental chemistry, and food chemistry.

IB Physics HL - Year one and two: A two-year, algebra-based introductory physics course. Requires 60 hours of laboratory work. The first year of the course includes, mostly, topics in classical mechanics. There is also a strong emphasis on the development of laboratory skills. The second year includes topics in electricity and magnetism, waves, optics, thermodynamics, and nuclear physics. The laboratory skills mastered during the first year of the course are used to complete the Internal Assessment IB requirements in the second year.

Scientific research III: focuses on individual students designing and implementing original research. Each student would require a certain expertise when using necessary lab equipment, performing techniques and knowing the safety issues associated with their individual project.

Scientific Research IV: Students will develop projects based on their interests. Projects at this level would be original in nature and will investigate students’ ideas to solve a particular problem. It is expected that the students will work with a university professor or in an industrial setting to find the answer to their research question. Students completing these projects are expected to present their solutions to the appropriate interests groups or in settings like the Best Robotics competitions, Siemens, the High School Engineering Competition, etc.
